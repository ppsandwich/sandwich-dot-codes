# SKILL_RECIPES_PPS.md

## Purpose

Use this skill whenever selecting, recommending, adapting, or writing recipes for Dylan to cook at home.

The goal is not just to find “good recipes”. The goal is to pick meals that match Dylan’s actual taste: big flavour, comforting structure, practical cooking time, and enough interest that dinner does not feel like a beige administrative task.

Dylan strongly prefers meals that take **one hour or less** from start to finish. Flavour matters much more than visual appeal. A dish can look like it lost a minor argument with a saucepan as long as it tastes excellent.

## Core cooking constraints

### Time

Default to recipes that take **60 minutes or less**, including prep and cooking.

Preferred timing bands:

- **Ideal:** 25–45 minutes.
- **Acceptable:** 45–60 minutes.
- **Use sparingly:** 60–75 minutes, only if the payoff is clearly worth it or the recipe is mostly passive.
- **Avoid by default:** slow cooks, roasts, long braises, overnight marinades, multi-stage weekend cooking, or anything requiring several hours.

Exception: Dylan clearly likes some slow-cooked and roasted flavour profiles, such as lamb shanks, braised beef, roast pork, roast lamb, red wine casseroles, pulled beef tacos, pork belly, and tagines. When using these ideas, adapt them into faster weeknight versions where possible: pressure-cooker-style logic, smaller cuts, mince, steaks, sausages, chicken thighs, tray bakes, pan sauces, or shortcut reductions.

### Available equipment

Assume Dylan has:

- Stove
- Microwave
- Oven
- Barbecue

Do not assume access to:

- Air fryer
- Slow cooker
- Pressure cooker
- Stand mixer
- Food processor
- Sous vide
- Smoker
- Deep fryer

If a recipe would normally use unavailable equipment, adapt it to the stove, oven, microwave, or barbecue. For example, convert air fryer recipes into oven tray bakes, pan-fried versions, or barbecue versions.

### Strong dislikes

- **Never include truffle or truffle oil.** Dylan hates truffles.
- Do not recommend recipes where truffle is a defining ingredient, even as an optional “luxury” flourish.
- Avoid precious restaurant styling or twee presentation instructions. Dylan cares far more about flavour than plating.

## Recipe selection rules

When choosing recipes for Dylan, prioritise meals that meet most of these criteria:

1. Cookable in 60 minutes or less.
2. Uses stove, oven, microwave, or barbecue.
3. Has a clear flavour hook: sauce, spice paste, glaze, gravy, cheese, herb dressing, salsa, yoghurt, tahini, or crisp texture.
4. Feels substantial enough for dinner.
5. Includes protein plus a satisfying base or side.
6. Can be made with common Australian supermarket ingredients.
7. Does not require high precision, specialist technique, or hard-to-find ingredients.
8. Produces leftovers or reheats well where possible.
9. Has enough flavour that it will not need rescuing at the table.
10. Avoids truffle completely.

## How to write recipes for Dylan

### Tone

Write recipes clearly and practically. Do not make the recipe sound like a lifestyle brand has trapped a person inside a chopping board.

Use plain, confident instructions. A little warmth is good. Forced cheerfulness is not.

Good tone:

- “Brown the chicken properly. This is where most of the flavour starts.”
- “The sauce should taste slightly too punchy before it goes over the rice. The rice will calm it down.”
- “If the pan looks dry, add a splash of water rather than pretending everything is fine.”

Avoid:

- “A delightful weeknight winner your whole family will adore.”
- “Elevate your dinner table.”
- “A symphony of flavours.”
- “Guilt-free comfort food.”
- “Restaurant-quality plating.”

### Structure

Use this structure unless the user asks for a different format:

1. Recipe name
2. One-sentence flavour summary
3. Time estimate
4. Serves
5. Equipment
6. Ingredients
7. Method
8. Optional swaps
9. Why this suits Dylan

Keep the method efficient. Mention where steps can overlap, such as cooking rice while making sauce, or roasting vegetables while preparing the protein.

### Recipe-writing principles

- Build flavour early: browning, toasting spices, caramelising onions, reducing sauces, roasting vegetables.
- Add acid at the end when useful: lemon, vinegar, yoghurt, pickles, salsa, or wine.
- Include salt guidance. Do not just say “season to taste” as if that solves everything.
- Offer supermarket-friendly swaps.
- Prefer flexible recipes over brittle ones.
- Make leftovers and reheating notes when relevant.
- Do not over-optimise for visual presentation. A garnish is useful only if it adds flavour or texture.
- Give doneness cues, not just times.
- Make the recipe resilient to normal home cooking chaos.

## Adaptation patterns

Use these patterns to convert appealing but slow recipes into practical meals.

### Slow braise → fast pan sauce or mince version

Instead of slow-cooked beef, lamb, or pork:

- Use mince, diced steak, sausages, chicken thighs, pork fillet, lamb backstrap, or thin steaks.
- Brown aggressively.
- Use stock, wine, tomato, miso, Worcestershire, soy, or mustard to create depth quickly.
- Simmer uncovered to reduce.
- Finish with butter, lemon, herbs, yoghurt, cheese, or gremolata.

### Roast dinner → tray bake

Instead of a full roast:

- Use smaller cuts: chicken thighs, drumsticks, salmon fillets, pork cutlets, sausages, lamb cutlets.
- Roast at higher heat.
- Add potatoes, pumpkin, carrots, fennel, capsicum, onion, zucchini, or broccoli.
- Finish with sauce: peppercorn, tahini, yoghurt, lemon butter, salsa, gravy, or pan juices.

### Air fryer → oven or barbecue

If a source recipe uses an air fryer:

- Use a hot oven on a lined tray.
- Use a barbecue for char where appropriate.
- Use a frying pan for crispy edges.
- Mention timing may be slightly longer than air fryer versions.

### Restaurant dish → supermarket dinner

Keep the flavour idea. Remove the theatre.

- Use accessible ingredients.
- Reduce garnish count.
- Avoid plating complexity.
- Preserve sauce, seasoning, texture, and comfort.

## Recommendation rubric

When evaluating a possible recipe, score it mentally against these factors:

- **Flavour intensity:** Is there a sauce, spice, char, cheese, acid, crunch, or savoury base?
- **Practicality:** Can Dylan make it in under an hour with his equipment?
- **Dinner satisfaction:** Does it feel like a proper meal?
- **Ingredient accessibility:** Can it be made from Coles/Woolworths-type ingredients?
- **Low faff:** Are the steps worth the result?
- **No truffle:** Absolute requirement.

A recipe with moderate effort and excellent flavour beats a beautiful, low-effort recipe that tastes like damp paperwork.
