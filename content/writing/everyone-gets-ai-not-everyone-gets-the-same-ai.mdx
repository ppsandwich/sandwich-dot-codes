## The democratic promise

AI is sold as the great leveller. Everyone gets a tutor, a coder, a designer, a researcher, a writing partner, a data analyst, and a tiny consultant in a box who occasionally lies with the confidence of a regional sales director.

The irritating thing is that this is not completely wrong.

A person with a cheap laptop and a monthly subscription can now do things that would have needed a team, a budget, or a suspiciously patient friend who "knows computers" a few years ago. That is not marketing fluff. It is genuinely strange.

You can go from vague idea to working prototype in an afternoon. Not a good prototype, necessarily. Quite possibly a haunted little stack of JavaScript and optimism. But still: something exists where previously there was only a note in your phone called "app idea???" that you would never open again. That matters.

A student can ask a model to explain something for the fifth time without feeling stupid. A small business can automate boring admin. A writer can get moving when the blank page is doing its little psychological warfare routine. Someone with no formal coding background can build a weird tool for a weird problem, which is basically the founding myth of half the useful software on earth.

So yes, AI gives ordinary people more capability, but "more capability" is doing a lot of work there.

More than what? More than before? More than each other? More than the people and companies already sitting on money, infrastructure, proprietary data, distribution, lawyers, cloud contracts, and internal tools?

That is where the democratic story starts to wobble.

Everyone gets AI; not everyone gets the same AI.

## The best intelligence is not equally available

The simplest divide is price.

There are free models. There are cheap models. There are fast little models that can summarise your meeting notes, rewrite an email, produce serviceable code, and confidently invent the name of a regulation that sounds plausible enough to ruin your afternoon.

They are useful (sometimes very useful).

But the most capable models tend to sit behind higher subscription tiers, API fees, usage caps, enterprise agreements, priority access, longer context windows, better tool use, stronger coding ability, faster inference, and integrations with actual business systems. The good version is not always the public version. The public version is often the version with a friendly chat box and a number of implied asterisks.

This is not surprising. Almost everything works this way. Transport. Healthcare. Education. Legal advice. Finance. Software. Coffee machines that cost more than my first car and still somehow require emotional management.

But AI is not being sold as "yet another productivity tool with a premium plan". It is being sold, or at least imagined, as a technology that flattens access to expertise. That is a much bigger promise. And it gets much stranger when the most powerful systems are not merely expensive, but withheld.

Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview is a useful example. Mythos has been described as a powerful unreleased frontier model with exceptional cybersecurity capabilities. Rather than releasing it as a public model, Anthropic put it inside Project Glasswing, a restricted initiative giving selected organisations access for defensive cybersecurity work.

That may be the responsible choice. A model that can find and exploit vulnerabilities is not exactly something you casually drop into the app store between a meditation timer and a suspiciously horny chess bot. "Now with autonomous exploit discovery" is not a feature you want next to in-app purchases.

Still, the decision reveals the shape of something larger.

The most powerful AI systems may not always become products. They may become private levers.

A model like Mythos does not need to be available to the public to matter. It can matter by being available to governments, security firms, infrastructure providers, banks, strategic partners, or a handful of companies whose names appear in press releases with a faint smell of procurement.

It can sit behind the wall. It can harden systems, find vulnerabilities, protect infrastructure, and give some institutions a level of capability ordinary users never touch.

That might be good. In some domains, public access would be reckless.

But it is not egalitarian in the cheerful keynote-slide sense.

The public gets the chatbot, the selected few get the machinery.

## The moat is not just the model

The other divide is data, which is less flashy because nobody unveils a dataset with a dramatic synth soundtrack.

Public models are powerful because they have absorbed a huge amount of general knowledge. They know a little bit about almost everything, which is useful in the same way a bright, sleep-deprived generalist is useful: impressive, fast, occasionally wrong in a way that makes you stare at the wall.

But general knowledge is only one layer.

The next advantage comes from private knowledge. Customer histories. Transaction records. Source code. Internal documents. Medical records. Legal archives. Product data. Logistics systems. Research databases. Expert-labelled examples. The boring, specific, expensive stuff that organisations have spent years accumulating, cleaning, neglecting, rediscovering, renaming, and storing in folders called "final_v7_real_final".

A general model can tell you what a decent retention strategy might look like.

A model connected to your actual customer behaviour can tell you which customers are likely to leave, what they are worth, what they have tried already, which intervention might work, and whether the support team accidentally trained them to hate you.

Those are not the same thing.

One is advice. The other is leverage.

This is where AI stops looking like a public library and starts looking like heavy machinery. The value is not only in the model. It is in what the model can see, what it is allowed to touch, and what it can do once it has an answer.

The open web is changing too. The first wave of large models benefited from a relatively open internet. Text, images, forums, documentation, journalism, code, creative work, old arguments, abandoned blogs, beautiful essays, broken Stack Overflow answers, and probably seventeen billion recipe introductions were all swept into the training furnace.

Now the web is growing teeth.

Publishers are pushing back. Creators are pushing back. Platforms are blocking crawlers. Infrastructure companies are giving site owners tools to restrict AI scraping. Content owners are demanding licensing deals. Lawsuits are trying to decide what was fair use, what was theft, and what was just "innovation" said confidently enough by someone wearing Allbirds.

This changes the economics of knowledge.

If future models depend more on licensed data, private partnerships, exclusive datasets, and negotiated access, then better AI will not necessarily come from having access to "the internet". It will come from having access to the right parts of the internet, under the right contracts, with the right lawyers, paid for by the right budget.

Negotiated access favours the people who can negotiate.

Funny how often that happens.

## Cheap AI may be everywhere. Decisive AI may not be.

The uncomfortable version of the AI future is not that AI disappears behind a locked door.

It is almost worse, because it will look more democratic than it is.

Basic AI may become cheap, common, and embedded in everything. It will sit inside search bars, phones, browsers, office software, email clients, design tools, spreadsheets, customer service systems, school platforms, shopping sites, and probably a toaster, because someone somewhere will decide breakfast needs "a conversational layer".

You will not need to go looking for AI. It will be there, like autocomplete with ambition.

So yes, everyone may get AI -- but decisive AI may be different.

The kind connected to proprietary data. The kind with high reliability. The kind that can act inside real systems. The kind tuned for specialised work. The kind with enterprise support, audit logs, security reviews, private deployment options, regulatory clearance, and a sales cycle long enough to develop moss.

That kind of AI is not just a clever assistant. It is infrastructure.

And infrastructure has owners.

This is why the simple version of the story does not work. AI can be democratising at the task level and unequal at the systems level.

At the task level, it helps individuals do more. It lowers the cost of learning, writing, coding, experimenting, prototyping, and making strange little tools for strange little problems. It gives people a bigger hammer. Sometimes that is enough to crack open a door that was previously welded shut.

At the systems level, scale bites back.

A large company can connect AI to private data, internal workflows, legal advice, customer systems, cloud infrastructure, and distribution. A government can use restricted models for national security. A bank can use AI to model risk. A tech company can use frontier systems internally before anyone else sees them. A security partner can use a model the public cannot touch.

None of this means AI is useless to ordinary people. That would be a silly conclusion, and I am trying to avoid collecting silly conclusions like fridge magnets.

It means the levelling effect has limits.

Some people will get the free assistant. Some will get the professional tool. Some will get the enterprise platform. Some will get the private model, trained on private data, running inside private infrastructure, aimed at private advantage.

And the rest of us will be told the future has been democratised because there is a chatbot in the search bar.

Which is not nothing.
But it is also not equality.
