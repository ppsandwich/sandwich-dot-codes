Retailers have spent the last decade trying to make stores measurable.

Online retail spoiled everyone. You can see the click, the scroll, the cart, the abandoned cart, the rage click, the suspiciously long hover over a product image. A physical shop is messier. People walk in, browse, pause, pick something up, put it back, drift around, then leave with a bottle of water and a private opinion about the price of cereal.

For retailers, that missing measurement layer has always been annoying. Foot traffic tells you someone entered. POS tells you what they bought. Cameras can estimate dwell, queues and demographics, but cameras are visible, expensive, legally awkward, and very good at making customers feel like they are being studied.

Now there is another option; Wi-Fi.

Not Wi-Fi as in “join our free network and receive marketing emails until the sun explodes”. Wi-Fi as a sensing layer. Wi-Fi as a way to read how radio waves move through a shop, bounce off bodies, and change when people walk, pause, gesture, breathe, or hover near the self-checkout wondering why the bagging area has once again chosen violence. This is where 802.11bf matters. 802.11bf is the Wi-Fi sensing standard. The basic idea is simple: Wi-Fi signals already move through shops, homes, offices and shopping centres. When those signals pass through a space, they change. Bodies interrupt them. Movement distorts them. Small motions leave patterns. Channel State Information, or CSI, captures some of that detail.

We have been able to measure CSI for years. The problem was that it was noisy, as raw sensor data often is. Technically full of information, practically full of screaming. AI changes that.

Machine learning can pull usable signal from the mess. It can help distinguish a person walking past a shelf from a group clustering near an endcap, a staff member restocking, a trolley moving through an aisle, or a customer standing in the tragic decision fog between two almost identical laundry detergents.

## This is not as futuristic as it sounds

Wi-Fi sensing sounds like a creepy future thing, but it is not really future technology anymore. The standard is here. The hardware is ordinary. The research is public. The cost is low enough that this is not just a Westfield, Amazon or defence-contractor problem.

A hobbyist can already experiment with Wi-Fi sensing using commodity hardware. Not a room full of lab gear. Cheap boards, Raspberry Pi-style setups, ESP32-class devices, normal Wi-Fi radios, open research, public code, and a weekend of questionable judgement. Projects like WiFi-DensePose showed the direction of travel: use Wi-Fi signal changes to infer human pose. Other research has explored people counting, room occupancy, activity recognition, breathing and heart-rate estimation.

![Real-time human pose and vital sign estimation using Wi-Fi signals. Image credit: RuView project on GitHub](/images/articles/ruview-wifi-densepose.jpg)

To be clear, this is not magic. It is not a perfect invisible camera. Many systems are fragile, lab-bound, or require careful calibration. A real store is a hostile environment full of metal shelving, fridges, glass, trolleys, children, reflective surfaces and one bloke standing in the cereal aisle for nine minutes. But retail analytics rarely starts with perfection. It starts with something directionally useful and cheap enough to deploy. Then it gets normalised. Then it gets benchmarked. Then someone puts it in a dashboard and acts like it was obvious. The retail use cases are not hard to imagine.

Measure dwell without cameras. Understand which aisles attract traffic but not conversion. Detect where shoppers slow down, double back, hesitate, cluster or avoid. Compare planograms. Measure queue formation. Test promotional displays. Understand the impact of music, lighting, signage, digital screens or product placement. That alone is useful.

Then it gets more uncomfortable.

Because Wi-Fi sensing is not limited to “someone is standing near the Tim Tams”. Research has shown that Wi-Fi signals can be used to estimate breathing and heart rate. That pushes the technology from store analytics into physiological sensing, which is where the room gets colder. A retailer could argue there are legitimate uses: detecting distress, identifying falls, improving safety in spaces where cameras are inappropriate, monitoring staff welfare, understanding queue stress, or measuring whether a store layout creates anxiety or congestion.

You can see the product roadmap writing itself, which is usually the first warning sign.

## The camera loophole problem

The privacy problem is not that Wi-Fi sensing is always worse than cameras. In some ways, it may be better. It does not capture faces. It does not record images of children. It can work in the dark. It can be designed to produce aggregate metrics rather than identifiable records. There are legitimate safety and accessibility use cases. 

That is the good version; the bad version is that it is tiny, cheap, passive, and easy to hide.

A camera announces itself. People broadly understand what it is. You see the dome on the ceiling and think, yes, fine, loss prevention is watching me buy toothpaste. A normal modern indignity. Wi-Fi sensing does not have that same visual language. An access point is just an access point. A little white square on the ceiling. A box behind a sign. A router under a counter. Nobody looks at one and thinks: this may be estimating my breathing rate.

That is the gap.

A lot of privacy law and customer expectation has grown around cameras, faces, recordings and personally identifiable data. Wi-Fi sensing does not fit neatly into those buckets. It may not produce an image. It may not identify a face. It may only generate “signals”, “events”, “patterns”, “aggregate analytics”, and other words people use when they want something invasive to sound like a spreadsheet. Australian privacy law still matters. If the data identifies someone, or could reasonably identify them, it can become personal information. If it relates to health, biometric characteristics, or physiological measurements, it may move into more sensitive territory.

But shoppers do not experience sensing in legal categories. They experience it as: the store measured something about my body without making that obvious.

If a supermarket measures average dwell time in the cereal aisle, most people will probably shrug. If it estimates whether shoppers’ breathing changes near locked cabinets, or whether heart rate rises in a checkout queue, the shrug may not survive. And customers should not need a networking degree to buy milk.

## Make it visible

There is a strong argument that Wi-Fi sensing should be visible.

Not because every use is sinister. Some uses could be genuinely useful. Better measurement could improve layouts, reduce queues, improve staffing, detect hazards and make stores more accessible. The issue is that this layer can be intimate without looking intimate. That is why signage matters. Not a legal footnote in 8-point type near the entrance. Real disclosure.

Something plain:

> This store uses Wi-Fi sensing to measure customer movement and occupancy. It does not record video.

And if physiological signals are inferred:

> This store uses wireless sensing technology that may detect movement and body signals such as breathing. Data is used for safety and store analytics.

Would that make customers uncomfortable?

Yes. Probably.

Good.

If a technology feels too creepy to explain clearly, that is not a communications problem. That is the product telling on itself. Australian retailers should not wait for legislation to force this. But legislation probably will need to catch up, because the incentives point the other way. Hidden measurement is cleaner. It avoids awkward conversations. It avoids customers opting out. It lets companies run pilots quietly and call them “operational analytics” until someone posts a photo on Reddit and everyone has a normal one.

At minimum, retailers using Wi-Fi sensing should disclose it clearly at store entry, explain what is being measured, state whether physiological signals are inferred, and offer a practical way to find out more. If the system can identify or track individuals, the threshold should be much higher. If it can infer health-related signals, consent should not be hand-waved into existence because someone walked past the bananas.

## The strange part is that AI made radio waves readable

The bigger story is not just retail. It is that AI does not only create new things. It makes old signals useful. Radio waves were already bouncing around the room. CSI was already measurable. The physical world was already leaving traces in the wireless environment. The missing piece was interpretation.

AI gives us that interpretation layer.

That should make us curious and slightly nervous, which is probably the correct emotional setting for 2026. What else is sitting in the noise? Could AI pull more detailed human activity from Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, UWB, 5G, RFID or other radio systems? Could it infer stress, fatigue, gait, falls, gestures, illness, sleep quality, attention, agitation or identity? Could stores understand not just where people went, but how their bodies responded to spaces, products, queues, prices, staff interactions and digital advertising?

Some of this will be overhyped. There will be vendor decks claiming “emotion-aware retail environments” and “frictionless human signal intelligence”, phrases that should legally require a small bell to ring. But not all of it will be nonsense - that is the awkward bit.

So how scared should people be?

Not “move to a cabin and line the walls with foil” scared. That way lies YouTube comments and poor ventilation. But more alert than they currently are. Wi-Fi sensing could become a useful layer of in-store measurement. It could help retailers make better stores. It could reduce reliance on cameras. It could measure spaces in ways that are cheaper, broader and less visually invasive than today’s systems.

It could also normalise a form of bodily measurement that customers cannot see, do not understand, and never meaningfully agreed to. The technology is here. It is getting easier. It is cheap enough to experiment with. AI has made the invisible parts of the wireless environment more readable than they used to be. Retailers will be tempted, because of course they will. Measurement is addictive. Once a business finds a new way to count something, it starts behaving as if the thing was always meant to be counted.

But not every measurable thing should quietly become a metric.

Especially when the thing being measured is a person walking through a shop, breathing.
