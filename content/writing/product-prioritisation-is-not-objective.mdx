Put enough product managers in a room, and eventually, someone will open a spreadsheet. 

We will list out twenty potential features. We will assign each one a score for Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort. We will multiply these numbers together to produce a final, undeniable RICE score. Then we will sort the list from highest to lowest and nod thoughtfully, comfortable in the knowledge that we have done the hard work of being objective.

Except, of course, we haven't. We have just built a machine for laundering our opinions into facts.

## The Illusion of the Calculator

There is a pervasive idea in product management that if we just find the right framework—RICE, ICE, MoSCoW, Kano, WSJF—we can eliminate the messy, human bias of decision-making. We want the framework to act as a calculator. We want to punch in the data and have it spit out the absolute truth of what we should build next.

But let's look at the actual data we punch into these calculators.

**Reach** is usually an optimistic guess based on a slice of users who vaguely clicked near a button once. 
**Impact** is a hope. It is how much we *want* the feature to matter.
**Confidence** is less a measure of certainty and more a measure of how good the product manager is at arguing. 
**Effort** is a polite fiction told by an engineering team that hasn't yet discovered the load-bearing legacy code lurking beneath the surface.

When you multiply four guesses together, you don't get an objective fact. You get a highly structured opinion.

## Do We Actually Want Objectivity?

If objective prioritisation is impossible, the natural next question is: what does "objective enough" look like? And more importantly, do companies actually want true objectivity?

Usually, they don't. True objectivity is terrifying. True objectivity might tell you that your CEO's pet project is mathematically useless, or that the feature promised to your biggest enterprise client is actually a negative-ROI distraction.

What companies actually want is not objectivity. They want a tidier way to justify difficult choices.

When you have limited engineering bandwidth and unlimited demands from sales, marketing, and executives, saying "no" based on gut feeling invites an argument. Saying "no" because the RICE score is 1.4 while the cutoff is 2.0 ends the argument. The spreadsheet acts as a shield. It takes the heat out of the rejection.

## The Spreadsheet is a Communication Tool

None of this means frameworks are useless. It just means we are using them wrong.

A prioritisation framework is not a decision-making engine. It is an alignment tool. The value of doing a RICE exercise isn't the number at the end; it's the conversation you have to get there.

If the lead engineer thinks Effort is an 8 and the PM thinks it's a 2, the framework has surfaced a massive misalignment in understanding. If sales thinks Impact is a 10 and product thinks it's a 3, you are building for different realities. The spreadsheet forces everyone to state their assumptions out loud.

Once you realise that the framework is just a structured way to argue, it becomes much more useful. You stop treating the final score as gospel. You use it to find the obvious wins and the obvious losers, and then you use your actual judgement—your messy, subjective, human judgement—to decide the middle.

## Objective Enough

You will never have perfect data. You will never have a flawless framework. And you will never eliminate bias from building products.

"Objective enough" means we argued about the right things. It means we understood the trade-offs before we made them. It means that when we are inevitably wrong about what to build, we at least understand *why* we were wrong, rather than just blaming the calculator.

The spreadsheet won't save you from making the wrong choice. But if you use it properly, it might help you make the wrong choice together.
