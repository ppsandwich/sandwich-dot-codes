Every time I sit down to build something for my portfolio, I face the same temptation: make it clean, make it professional, make it look like every other developer portfolio out there. Dark mode. Sans-serif font. Grid of project cards. Maybe a subtle gradient.

And every time, I resist.

Not because there's anything wrong with clean design. Clean design is hard to do well. But because **my portfolio should feel like me**, and I am not a clean, minimal, corporate-speak kind of person.

## The Problem with Professionalism

There's a pervasive idea in tech that "professional" means "sterile." That your portfolio should look like it was designed by the same agency that did a SaaS dashboard. That personality is risky.

I think the opposite is true. **Personality is memorable.** The portfolio that makes someone say "what the hell is this?" and then spend 10 minutes exploring is infinitely more valuable than the one that gets a polite nod and a closed tab.

## What Play Produces

When you build things for fun — without worrying about whether they'll impress a hiring manager — you learn faster. You try weirder techniques. You combine technologies in unexpected ways. You make mistakes that lead to discoveries.

My Pixel Garden project started because I wondered "what if cellular automata could evolve?" My Voice Canvas came from a late-night experiment with the Web Audio API. Neither was planned as a portfolio piece. Both taught me more than any tutorial.

## The Sandwich Philosophy

I call my approach "sandwich codes" because:

1. **Layers matter.** A good sandwich has texture, contrast, surprise. So does good software.
2. **It's handmade.** You can taste the difference between a hand-assembled sandwich and one made by a machine. Users can feel the difference between handcrafted software and template-driven development.
3. **It's not precious.** Nobody agonizes over the perfect sandwich. You make it, you eat it, you make a different one next time. Software should be the same — build it, ship it, learn from it, build something else.

## Build Weird Things

If you're reading this and you have a half-finished weird project sitting in a repo somewhere, finish it. Ship it. Don't polish it until it's indistinguishable from everything else. Let it be strange. Let it be yours.

The world has enough clean portfolios. It needs more weird ones.
