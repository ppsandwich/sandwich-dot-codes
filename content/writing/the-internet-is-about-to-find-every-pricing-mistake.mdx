Many retailers probably do not know how many incorrect prices they publish today.

A staff member notices a product that looks unusually cheap, fixes it in the system and moves on. A customer reports a mismatch. A store manager removes a ticket. An ecommerce team corrects a promotion before many orders arrive. Unless the error causes a major loss or customer complaint, it may never be formally recorded.

That makes the existing risk difficult to quantify. Retailers may know about the large incidents, but not the steady flow of smaller mistakes being silently corrected by staff.

Agentic shopping could make those mistakes much harder to contain.

AI shopping assistants can monitor large numbers of products, compare prices across retailers and react when an item falls well below its normal range. As these tools become more common, a price that might previously have remained unnoticed for hours could be identified and acted on almost immediately.

This will affect large retailers, but smaller businesses may face the greatest risk. They are more likely to rely on manual uploads, spreadsheets, basic ecommerce platforms and small teams. They may also lack formal pricing controls, real-time monitoring and the ability to absorb hundreds of orders placed at the wrong price.

## From catalogues to automated fulfilment

Printed catalogues had several layers between a pricing mistake and a completed sale.

Product and marketing teams prepared the offer. Designers placed it into the catalogue. Other staff reviewed the pages. After publication, a customer still had to see the offer, travel to a store, find the item and take it to a register. Staff could identify an obvious error before the transaction was completed.

Ecommerce removed many of those checks.

Prices now move from ERPs, supplier feeds, spreadsheets, promotion engines and bulk-editing tools directly onto public websites. Orders can then be accepted, paid for and sent to a warehouse without human review.

The systems are working as designed. The problem is that they can process an incorrect price as efficiently as a correct one.

Community sites such as OzBargain increased the speed at which mistakes could spread. A user could find an unusually low price, post it, and send a large number of customers to the retailer within minutes.

Agentic shopping increases that capability again. It replaces occasional human discovery with continuous automated monitoring.

A shopping agent can compare a product with its historical price, competing retailers, other variants and the likely market value. It does not need to recognise with certainty that a price is wrong. It only needs to recognise that the price is unusually attractive.

If many agents are doing this at once, every public catalogue becomes subject to constant automated review.

## Why are pricing controls still limited?

The technology needed to detect many pricing errors is relatively straightforward.

A system could flag prices that:

- fall below product cost or a minimum margin
- differ sharply from recent price history
- sit far outside the normal range for the category
- create unexpected results when promotions and coupons are combined
- differ significantly between similar variants
- produce an abnormal increase in order velocity

Commerce platforms are already adding AI tools for product descriptions, merchandising, search, personalisation and customer service. Pre-publication price anomaly detection is less prominent.

There are practical reasons for this.

A commerce platform may not have access to supplier funding, costs, margins, clearance plans or offline prices. An unusual price may be intentional. Retailers also change large numbers of prices, and a system that generates too many warnings will quickly be ignored.

Responsibility is fragmented as well. Pricing may pass through an ERP, a pricing platform, an ecommerce system, a marketplace feed and a promotion engine. Each vendor can reasonably claim that another part of the stack is responsible for validating it.

None of this makes the problem impossible to solve.

Retailers already use anomaly detection for fraud, payments, stock management and demand forecasting. Pricing systems could apply similar controls, with the level of review based on the potential risk.

A small change to a low-value item may require no intervention. A 90 per cent reduction on an expensive product should probably require secondary approval.

For smaller retailers, this may need to be built into mainstream commerce platforms rather than sold as another enterprise product. A small business is unlikely to build its own pricing-risk model, but it may be seriously affected by a single spreadsheet or decimal error.

## The blast radius is increasing

Australian Consumer Law does not mean every obvious online pricing mistake must automatically be honoured. The outcome depends on the circumstances, the retailer's terms and when a contract is formed. Retailers commonly reserve the right to cancel orders caused by genuine errors.

That does not remove the commercial consequences.

Cancelling a small number of orders may produce a few complaints. Cancelling thousands can create payment reversals, support demand, negative media coverage and reputational damage. A retailer may decide to honour some orders or provide compensation even when it is not legally required.

Shopping agents reduce the time available to respond. Orders may be placed before a staff member sees the change, and automated fulfilment may reserve or dispatch stock before the incident is escalated.

Retailers may respond by adding more controls throughout the pricing and ordering process:

- secondary approval for high-risk price changes
- minimum-margin rules
- automated promotion-stacking tests
- quantity limits on unusually discounted products
- short fulfilment holds when demand rises unexpectedly
- more personalised or account-specific offers
- fewer public, deeply discounted promotions

The final two responses could reduce price transparency. If public discounts become easier for bots and agents to find and exploit, retailers may prefer offers restricted to loyalty members, apps, targeted audiences or unique coupon codes.

This would reduce the risk of an automated rush, but it would also make comparison shopping harder.

## Prices now have an audience of machines

Retail has progressively reduced the distance between a published price and a completed transaction.

Catalogues created awareness. Ecommerce made products immediately purchasable. Bargain communities made exceptional prices easier to discover. Agentic shopping can combine discovery, comparison and purchase into one automated process.

This is useful when the price is correct. When it is not, each stage removes another opportunity for human review.

Retailers should assume that public prices will increasingly be read by machines more often than by people. Those machines can monitor continuously, remember historical prices and react within seconds.

The risk is not limited to retailers with outdated systems. Highly automated businesses may be particularly exposed because publishing, checkout and fulfilment can all operate successfully around one incorrect number.

Retailers need better measurement of the errors occurring today, including those quietly corrected before they become incidents. Commerce platforms should also provide accessible anomaly detection before prices are published, particularly for the smaller businesses least able to build it themselves.

Before using AI to generate another product description, retailers may have a more immediate use for it: checking whether the price is plausible.
