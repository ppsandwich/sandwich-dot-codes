## Overview

JIWW (Jump In, Water's Wet) is a web app that generates complete, production-ready MIDI song arrangements from a single genre selection. You pick one of 16 genres — house, techno, drum & bass, hip-hop, lo-fi, synthwave, jazz fusion, and more — click "Jump In," and the app creates a full multi-track MIDI song with tempo, swing, instrument palette, arrangement structure, melodic hooks, verses, choruses, and dense musical layering.

The output is designed to be immediately usable. Import the downloaded MIDI stems into any DAW and you'll hear a structured, genre-appropriate song idea — not a random loop, not a single pattern, but a complete arrangement with intro, verses, choruses, breakdown, and outro. Each track uses General MIDI-compatible instruments, so playback works out of the box.

## The Problem It Solves

Most MIDI generation tools produce loops. You get a 4-bar drum pattern or a chord progression, and you're left to arrange, layer, and develop it yourself. That's useful, but it's not a song. The gap between a generated loop and a full arrangement is hours of manual work — choosing instruments, writing basslines, building transitions, adding hooks, varying sections, and managing density across verses and choruses.

JIWW fills that gap. It generates complete songs: 8 to 16 tracks, 64 to 128 bars, with distinct sections that build and release energy. The arrangement has a beginning, middle, and end. The chorus has a hook. The bassline moves. The drums have fills and transitions. It's a song sketch, not a building block.

The target user is a music producer who wants a quick starting point. They don't need to make every creative decision from scratch — they need something structured enough to react to, modify, and develop into a finished track.

## How It Works

### Generation Pipeline

The generation engine is a modular pipeline that runs deterministically from a seed. Given the same genre and seed, it produces the same song every time. The pipeline has 14 stages:

1. **Genre profile selection** — Load the genre's BPM range, swing range, instrument pools, chord templates, drum templates, and arrangement templates.
2. **Global song parameters** — Choose BPM, swing amount, key, scale, time signature, and song length from the genre's allowed ranges.
3. **Arrangement generation** — Build the song structure: intro, verse A, chorus A, verse B, chorus B, bridge, final chorus, outro. Each section has a bar count and energy level.
4. **Track selection** — Choose 4 to 16 instruments from the genre's instrument pool, ensuring at least one rhythm, one bass, one harmonic, and one melodic track.
5. **Harmony generation** — Create chord progressions for the song, with section-level variation.
6. **Rhythm foundation** — Generate drum and percussion patterns with genre-specific groove, fills, and transitions.
7. **Bass generation** — Create basslines with root movement, syncopation, and section-specific intensity.
8. **Chord track generation** — Render chord voicings with inversions, rhythmic comping, and density changes.
9. **Melodic hook generation** — Create at least one memorable hook using motif logic: ascending/descending contours, call-and-response phrases, repetition with variation.
10. **Supporting parts** — Generate counter-melodies, pads, arpeggios, and texture layers.
11. **Transitions and fills** — Add section transitions, risers, and drum fills.
12. **Humanization** — Apply velocity variation and micro-timing offsets for a natural feel.
13. **MIDI rendering** — Convert the internal representation to General MIDI-compatible `.mid` files.
14. **ZIP packaging** — Bundle all tracks with metadata into a downloadable ZIP.

### Song Structure

Every generated song uses a complete arrangement template. The minimum structure:

- **Intro** — Low energy, sparse instrumentation, sets the tonal palette
- **Verse A** — Moderate density, space for vocals or lead development
- **Chorus A** — Full hook, more active tracks, wider voicings
- **Verse B** — Variation on Verse A, slight density increase
- **Chorus B** — Variation on Chorus A, additional layers
- **Bridge / Breakdown** — Energy drop, harmonic departure, textural shift
- **Final Chorus** — Maximum density, all tracks active, widest register
- **Outro** — Energy wind-down, sparse ending

Electronic genres get longer intros and outros (8–16 bars). Pop and hip-hop genres use more compact arrangements (4–8 bar sections).

### Track Generation

Each song contains 4 to 16 instrument tracks drawn from genre-specific pools. Every track has a role:

- **Drums** — Kick, snare, hats, percussion, fills, transitions
- **Bass** — Root movement, syncopation, octave variation
- **Chords** — Voicings, inversions, rhythmic comping
- **Melody / Hook** — Motifs, call-and-response, repetition with variation
- **Counter-melody** — Complementary phrases that respond to the hook
- **Pad** — Sustained harmonies, textural support
- **Arpeggio** — Rhythmic note patterns from the chord tones
- **FX** — Risers, hits, transitions, atmosphere

Every track includes section-level participation — tracks mute and unmute across the arrangement to create density variation between verses and choruses.

### MIDI Quality

Generated MIDI is designed to be musically useful, not random. Each track includes:

- Humanized velocity (controlled random variation, not uniform)
- Humanized timing (micro-offsets within genre-appropriate limits)
- Genre-correct rhythm patterns
- Section-level variation (verse patterns differ from chorus patterns)
- Repetition with small changes (motifs evolve, not loop exactly)
- Harmonic alignment with the chord progression
- Proper bar and beat alignment

Melodic tracks use motif logic: a short rhythmic and melodic cell that repeats, varies, and develops across sections. Hooks appear in choruses and are hinted at before the first chorus.

## Technology

JIWW is built for Vercel Serverless. The entire generation pipeline runs synchronously within a serverless API route — no background workers, no persistent compute, no long-running processes.

### Stack

- **Next.js** with React for the frontend and API routes
- **TypeScript** for type-safe generation logic
- **Tailwind CSS** for styling
- **Web Audio API** with General MIDI soundfont for browser-based preview playback
- **JSZip** for in-memory ZIP generation
- **Custom MIDI renderer** for generating `.mid` files from internal representation

### Serverless Design

The generation engine is designed around serverless constraints:

- All MIDI files are generated in memory — no filesystem writes
- ZIP files are generated in memory using JSZip
- Generation completes within typical Vercel serverless execution limits
- Songs are kept between 64 and 128 bars to control output size
- Tracks are capped at 16 to limit processing time
- The generation pipeline is deterministic and efficient — same seed produces same output

For the MVP, the frontend may encode the generated song payload client-side and generate download files in the browser, avoiding the need for persistent server-side storage.

### Deterministic Generation

Every song is generated from a seed. The seed can be user-provided or auto-generated. Given the same genre and seed, the pipeline produces identical output every time. This means users can share seeds to reproduce specific songs.

### Preview Playback

The app uses the Web Audio API with a General MIDI soundfont loaded on demand. Users can preview the full song (all tracks together) or solo-preview individual tracks. Playback controls include play, pause, stop, restart, timeline position, and BPM display.

Soundfonts are lazy-loaded to keep the initial page load fast.

## What I Learned

- Deterministic generation from a seed is essential for reproducibility, but getting musical randomness to feel natural within deterministic constraints is the hard part. The seed controls everything, but the output shouldn't feel robotic.
- General MIDI is the right choice for preview and export. It's universally compatible with DAWs, sounds acceptable for preview, and keeps the output format simple. No audio rendering, no sample libraries, no format conversion.
- Serverless constraints force good engineering. Generating everything in memory, capping song length, limiting track count, and avoiding filesystem writes all lead to a cleaner, more portable architecture.
- The arrangement is what makes the output feel like a song, not a loop. Without distinct sections — verse, chorus, bridge — with different energy levels and track participation, the output would just be a long repeating pattern.
- Hook generation using motif logic (short cells that repeat, vary, and develop) produces more musical results than random note generation. The hook needs to be rhythmically distinct from the bass and chord parts, resolve against the harmony, and repeat enough to be memorable.

## Challenges

- **Musical quality within constraints**: Generating MIDI that sounds like a human wrote it — with appropriate rhythm, harmony, melody, and arrangement — while running entirely in a serverless function with no external AI services.
- **Genre differentiation**: Making 16 genres feel genuinely different required deep genre-specific profiles with distinct BPM ranges, swing amounts, instrument pools, chord templates, drum patterns, and arrangement styles.
- **Serverless execution limits**: Keeping generation fast enough to complete within Vercel's serverless timeout while producing 64–128 bars of 4–16 tracks of MIDI data.
- **Preview performance**: Loading General MIDI soundfonts in the browser and playing back multi-track arrangements in sync without blocking the UI thread.
- **Arrangement density**: Balancing track count, section energy, and layering so that verses feel sparse and choruses feel full without the output sounding cluttered or empty.
