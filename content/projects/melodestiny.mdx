## Overview

Melodestiny is a web-based song lyric analysis tool that scores pop songs on structural and lyrical quality. You paste in a song title and lyrics, hit Analyze, and get a weighted score out of 100 with a detailed breakdown across 25 algorithmic techniques — each one derived from the playbooks of legendary songwriters like Max Martin, Diane Warren, Jack Antonoff, Pharrell Williams, Julia Michaels, Ed Sheeran, Ryan Tedder, Sia, Benny Blanco, Ilsey Juber, and Linda Perry.

The results include a wax-seal-style score badge, an interactive technique score grid, a collapsible breakdown table, highlighted lyrics with inline annotations, syllable boundary markers, and hoverable line-level feedback badges. It also offers client-side AI lyric generation using OpenRouter, and triggers to export raw lyrics or detailed feedback reports. The entire analysis runs client-side in the browser via a Rust-compiled WebAssembly module — no server, no API calls, no latency.

The design is intentionally anachronistic: parchment backgrounds, serif typography featuring the calligraphic display font **Mustopha**, muted earth tones, and hand-drawn-style dividers. The idea is that analyzing lyrics should feel like opening a well-loved leather journal in a Victorian songwriter's study, not staring at a dashboard.

## The Problem It Solves

There are plenty of lyric annotation sites (Genius, Musixmatch) and plenty of songwriting advice scattered across YouTube videos and masterclasses. What's missing is a tool that takes the actual craft knowledge embedded in those masterclasses — the structural rules, the rhythmic patterns, the vocabulary choices — and applies them algorithmically to your lyrics in seconds.

Songwriters get feedback from co-writers, producers, and A&R reps, but that feedback is subjective, slow, and expensive. Melodestiny doesn't replace that feedback. It gives you a structural X-ray of your lyrics before you take them to anyone else. Does the chorus arrive early enough? Is the hook memorable through repetition? Are the line lengths consistent enough to sing? Does the bridge introduce new vocabulary or just recycle the verses?

These are questions with measurable answers, and Melodestiny measures them.

### Highlighted Lyrics, Syllables, and Annotation Popovers

The lyrics display in **View Analysis** mode is highly interactive, transforming the plain text input into a rich typographical report:
- **Underlines and Annotations**: Every flagged line or phrase is underlined based on its significance:
  - **Gold underline** — Positive flag (technique applied well)
  - **Rust underline** — Warning flag (improvement opportunity)
  - **Blue-grey dashed underline** — Neutral note/tip
- **Multi-Annotation Support**: For text segments that trigger multiple songwriting techniques, highlights align cleanly. Tapping the segment opens a multi-annotation popover listing all matching tips, featuring a count badge (e.g., `3 Notes`) and custom scroll list grouping.
- **Syllable Division Markers & Counts**: Word syllables are split using the onset-principle and marked visually with subtle bullet separators (e.g., `Mel·o·des·ti·ny`). Each line displays its final syllable count (e.g., `8s`) at the right margin.
- **Line-Level Feedback Badges**: If a line contains feedback, a hoverable/tappable `(i)` badge appears next to the syllable count—colored gold for positive highlights or red for warnings—revealing specific suggestions instantly.

### Advanced Section Detection & Auto-Partitioning

Melodestiny automatically structures raw song lyrics into sections. It parses explicit square brackets (e.g., `[Chorus]`), colons (e.g., `Verse 1:`), and common songwriting keywords.
If no labels are present, a structural auto-partitioning algorithm segments the lyrics based on spacing patterns and repetition, labeling sections (e.g., `[Intro]`, `[Verse]`, `[Chorus]`, `[Outro]`) dynamically and flagging the UI with a notice recommending manual labels for optimal accuracy. Section titles are filtered out during calculation so they do not skew syllable or line-length averages.

### AI Lyric Generator (Best-of-Three)

A built-in AI lyric generation drawer integrates with OpenRouter. Users provide their own API key and choose from top models (like Claude 3.5 Sonnet or GPT-4o). The system uses a **Best-of-Three** workflow:
1. Generates three distinct variations in parallel.
2. Automatically passes all three candidates into the client-side Rust-compiled WebAssembly engine.
3. Compares the scores and injects the highest-scoring candidate directly into the editor for further refining.

### State Persistence, Reset, and Demo Hits

- **State Persistence**: Song titles and lyrics are automatically saved to `localStorage` as the user types, protecting creative progress across tab closures or accidental refreshes.
- **Application Reset**: A header-level reset button clears local storage and restores the clean, blank editor state immediately.
- **Demo Song Loader**: When the editor is blank, a prominent `✨ Load Demo Song` overlay lets users load one of 10 pre-loaded historical #1 pop hits (e.g., Max Martin-produced chart-toppers) with a single click to instantly test the tool's capabilities.

### Lyrical & Report Export

Users can download exports in two formats:
1. **Clean Raw Lyrics (TXT)**: Formatted copy of the text containing the latest lyric edits, removing all analysis highlights and syllable separator dots.
2. **Detailed Feedback Report (TXT/MD)**: A structured report containing the final wax-seal score, the 25-technique breakdown table, and a categorized list of high, medium, and low priority improvement opportunities complete with original line references.

### Key Algorithms

**Syllable counting and splitting** uses a multi-layered rule system. The vowel-group method handles base syllables, supplemented with special handling for vowel clusters, silent endings, and consonant splits:

```rust
fn count_syllables(word: &str) -> usize {
    let word = word.to_lowercase();
    let vowels: Vec<char> = "aeiouy".chars().collect();
    let mut count = 0;
    let mut prev_was_vowel = false;

    // 1. Count vowel groups
    for ch in word.chars() {
        let is_vowel = vowels.contains(&ch);
        if is_vowel && !prev_was_vowel {
            count += 1;
        }
        prev_was_vowel = is_vowel;
    }

    // 2. Adjust for magic-e pattern (consonant-vowel-consonant-e)
    if word.ends_with('e') && count > 1 {
        // Special case: check if it's not a "-le" ending preceded by a consonant
        let chars: Vec<char> = word.chars().collect();
        if chars.len() >= 2 && chars[chars.len() - 2] != 'l' {
            count -= 1;
        }
    }

    // 3. Adjust for silent -ed (e.g. "walked", but not "started")
    if word.ends_with("ed") && count > 1 {
        let chars: Vec<char> = word.chars().collect();
        if chars.len() >= 3 {
            let prev_char = chars[chars.len() - 3];
            if prev_char != 't' && prev_char != 'd' {
                count -= 1;
            }
        }
    }

    count.max(1)
}
```

For displaying visual dividers, the **syllable splitting** algorithm maps words by locating vowel nuclei, adjusting for silent trailing vowels, and applying the **onset principle** (consonants preceding a vowel group are grouped with that vowel's syllable, and multiple medial consonants split after the first consonant).

**Rhyme detection** operates in two tiers. It first queries the compiled CMU Pronouncing Dictionary subset for phonetic endings (stressed vowel and trailing phonemes, e.g., `"love"` -> `"AH V"`). If a word isn't in the subset, the engine falls back to heuristic matching: stripping common suffixes, checking vowel endings, and identifying common spelling rhyme pairs (e.g., `ee` and `ea`).

**Structural advisory (T15)** maps the song sections (e.g., `[Intro]`, `[Verse]`, `[Chorus]`, `[Bridge]`, `[Outro]`) to evaluate arrangement completeness. It checks for:
- Missing bridges (flagged if there are $\geq 2$ verses and $\geq 2$ choruses but no bridge).
- Missing pre-choruses (if line count ratio between verse and chorus exceeds $1.5$ or is below $0.67$).
- Structural errors (like back-to-back consecutive verses without chorus relief, or ending on a verse/bridge instead of the chorus payoff).

**Singability metrics** flag lines with dense consonant clusters (> 0.6 density) or very long syllables (> 4 characters per syllable) — the kinds of lines that are hard to sing a cappella walking down the street.

## Technology

The entire analysis pipeline runs client-side. No serverless functions, no API calls, no data sent anywhere. The frontend is Next.js with Tailwind CSS; the engine is Rust compiled to WebAssembly.

### Rust and WebAssembly

Rust was the right choice for the analysis engine for three reasons: performance (500 lines of lyrics analyzed in under 200ms), portability (the same crate compiles to WASM for the browser and can be wrapped in Tauri for native iOS/Android later), and correctness (the type system catches the kind of off-by-one errors that plague algorithmic text processing).

The WASM module bundles static data — a top-5000 English word frequency list derived from Google Books n-gram data and a 2000-word CMU Pronouncing Dictionary subset — so it's self-contained. No external lookups are required at runtime.

### Next.js and TypeScript

The frontend is a single-page Next.js app using the App Router. TypeScript is used throughout, with types defined once in Rust and translated to TypeScript interfaces. Tailwind CSS handles styling with a custom design token system for the antique aesthetic.

## UI Design

The interface is a single-view, responsive layout. On mobile, input elements stack above results; on desktop, a two-column layout features independent scrolling, keeping the results sticky on the right. 

A custom `AnnotatedTextarea` component toggles the input field between **Edit Lyrics** (standard editing textarea) and **View Analysis** (interactive text displaying syllable markers, boundary divisions, hoverable feedback badges, and underlined annotations). The score badge is rendered as a wax-seal-style circular element that scales in with a spring overshoot animation. A 5×3 grid (desktop) or 3×5 grid (mobile) presents progress bars for all 25 technique scores. 

A client-side **Dark Mode** toggle switches the workspace to an organic, low-contrast slate-950 and gold theme, optimizing borders and text contrast for low-light studies.

## What I Learned

- **WASM bundle size is a real constraint.** Bundling a 2000-word phonetic dictionary and a 5000-word frequency list with the Rust crate means the WASM binary pushes toward the 2MB gzip target. Stripping debug symbols, using `wasm-opt`, and compressing the static data kept it under budget.
- **Client-side LLM coordination requires flexibility.** Integrating an AI Lyric Generator in the browser using OpenRouter requires prompting users for their own API keys and handling network requests gracefully. The **Best-of-Three** workflow runs parallel queries, meaning the UI needs to manage multiple loading states smoothly.
- **Section detection from plain text is harder than it sounds.** Users paste lyrics in wildly different formats — some label sections with brackets or colons, some don't, and some include timestamps or chord notation. The preprocessing pipeline filters out non-lyric data and maps coordinates, but the spacing-based auto-partitioning fallback is crucial for raw inputs.
- **Syllable splitting in English has massive edge cases.** Heuristics like the vowel-group method are approximate. Adjusting for magic-e, silent -ed endings, and consonant splits required layered exceptions in the Rust engine to avoid miscounts on common pop words (like "walked" or "moved").
- **Designing for an antique aesthetic without sacrificing usability is a balancing act.** Serif fonts, custom display typefaces like Mustopha, and textured backgrounds can tank readability if contrast ratios aren't managed. Every color combination, including the dark mode palette, was validated against WCAG AA thresholds.

## Challenges

- **Aligning overlays on editable text:** Developing the custom `AnnotatedTextarea` component required overlapping the text layers so highlights align exactly with the editable textarea's scroll position and font sizes.
- **Rhyme detection without a full phonetic dictionary:** The CMU Pronouncing Dictionary is too large (~134,000 entries) to bundle entirely. Combining a ~2000-word subset of common pop vocabulary with a heuristic suffix/diphthong fallback was necessary to stay within WebAssembly bundle limits.
- **Multi-annotation overlap management:** When a single line or word triggers multiple techniques (e.g. repetition and pronoun density), stacking highlights became messy. Merging overlaps into a unified popup with count badges and list layouts kept the UI clean.
- **Animation performance with many annotations:** A song with 50+ flagged lines means 50+ annotation underlines animating in sequence. Using CSS `@keyframes` with `animation-delay` rather than JavaScript-driven animations kept everything on the compositor thread.
